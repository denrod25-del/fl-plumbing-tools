A tankless (on-demand) water heater heats water as it flows, instead of keeping 40 to 50 gallons hot around the clock. For the larger, newer homes common in Palm Beach Gardens, that can mean endless hot water for back-to-back showers, a reclaimed corner of the garage, and a unit that often outlasts a conventional tank.

Why this matters in Palm Beach Gardens

Palm Beach Gardens has been a county growth leader, with master-planned communities like Avenir and Alton bringing a wave of upscale new construction. These are exactly the households — multiple bathrooms, busy mornings, owners who care about efficiency and resale — where a tankless conversion tends to make sense. Unlike older parts of the county, the demand here isn’t about failing pipe; it’s about upgrading comfort and efficiency in homes that are already in good shape.

Why owners convert to tankless

  • Endless hot water — no running out mid-shower when the household is all up at once.
  • Space savings — a wall-mounted unit frees the floor space a tank occupies.
  • Longer service life — well-maintained tankless units often outlast tank heaters.
  • Energy efficiency — no standby losses from keeping a tank hot all day.
  • Leak risk — no large tank sitting full of water waiting to fail and flood.

Gas vs. electric — the Florida decision

Gas tankless

Gas units deliver the high flow rates that larger PBG homes want, supporting multiple fixtures at once. They need an adequate gas supply line and proper venting, so a conversion from an electric tank may involve added gas and venting work. Communities differ in gas availability, so this is confirmed home by home.

Electric tankless

Electric units are compact and simpler to vent, but they draw heavily on the electrical panel and are generally better suited to smaller hot-water demand or point-of-use applications. An electrical service upgrade is sometimes required.

Florida sizing advantage

Tankless sizing depends on flow rate and how far the unit must raise the water temperature. Florida’s warm incoming water means a smaller temperature rise than in colder climates, so a correctly sized gas unit can comfortably serve a busy multi-bath home. A plumber sizes the unit from your fixtures and usage patterns.

Hard water & descaling

Palm Beach County water tends to be hard, and scale is a tankless heater’s main enemy: it coats the heat exchanger and erodes efficiency. Plan for periodic descaling (flushing) as routine maintenance, and consider pairing the unit with a water softener to slow scale buildup and protect the investment.

What affects the cost

  • Gas vs. electric and whether new gas line or electrical capacity is needed
  • Venting requirements for a gas unit
  • Unit size / flow rate for your household
  • Relocation if the new unit moves to a different spot
  • Water softener or descaling kit add-ons
  • Permit and inspection fees through your local building department

Permits & who can do the work

Water heater replacement and tankless conversion are permitted work in Florida. In Palm Beach Gardens the permit and inspections run through the City of Palm Beach Gardens building department; nearby unincorporated areas fall under Palm Beach County’s building division (the “authority having jurisdiction,” or AHJ). A Florida state-licensed plumbing contractor normally pulls the permit and schedules inspections; gas and electrical changes may bring in additional licensed trades. Confirm current permit fees and requirements with your specific AHJ — they vary and change over time.

Frequently asked questions

Is a tankless water heater worth it in Palm Beach Gardens?

Many Palm Beach Gardens owners value the endless hot water, the reclaimed floor space, and the longer service life a tankless unit can offer versus a tank.

Whether it pays off depends on your hot-water demand, your fuel (gas vs. electric), and installation specifics. A licensed plumber can model it for your home.

Gas or electric tankless for a PBG home?

Gas tankless units deliver high flow rates and suit larger homes with multiple bathrooms, but they need adequate gas supply and proper venting. Electric tankless units are simpler to install but require significant electrical capacity and are usually better for smaller demand.

Newer Palm Beach Gardens communities such as Avenir and Alton vary in what is available, so the right choice depends on your home’s existing gas and electrical service.

How is a tankless unit sized?

Sizing is based on how many fixtures you might run at once (flow rate in gallons per minute) and the temperature rise needed. Florida’s warm incoming water actually helps — the unit doesn’t have to heat as far as it would up north — so a properly sized gas unit can supply a busy household. A plumber calculates this from your fixtures and habits.

Does hard water hurt a tankless heater?

Yes. Scale from hard water builds up on a tankless unit’s heat exchanger and reduces efficiency over time. Periodic descaling (flushing) is recommended maintenance, and pairing the unit with a water softener can reduce scale and protect the investment, which matters in hard-water Palm Beach County.

How long does a tankless conversion take?

A straightforward swap can often be done in a day, but converting from a tank to gas tankless may add gas-line and venting work, and electric conversions may need an electrical upgrade. Your plumber can give a schedule after assessing the existing setup.

Do I need a permit to install a tankless water heater?

Yes — water heater replacement and conversion are permitted work. Your licensed plumbing contractor typically pulls the permit through the City of Palm Beach Gardens (or Palm Beach County for unincorporated areas) and schedules inspections. Gas and electrical changes may involve additional trades. Verify current fees with your AHJ.
