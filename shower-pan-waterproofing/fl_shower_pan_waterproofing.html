📊 Liner System Comparison
PVC Liner (30 mil) STANDARD
• Most common in FL new construction
• Compatible with clamping ring drains
• Folds corners — requires no-crease technique
• Cost: $0.50–1.20/sq ft material
• Life: 20–30 years in FL conditions
PVC Liner (40 mil) UPGRADED
• Preferred for large showers (>36 sq ft)
• Better puncture resistance
• Code required for steam showers
• Cost: $1.00–2.00/sq ft material
• Life: 30–40 years
CPE Liner (40 mil) ALTERNATIVE
• Chlorinated polyethylene — better chemical resistance
• Does NOT bond to mortar bed like PVC
• Preferred by some FL inspectors for low-VOC projects
• Cost: $1.50–2.50/sq ft
• Life: 25–35 years
Hot Mop (4-ply felt) TRADITIONAL
• Applied hot at job site — no seams
• Requires licensed waterproofing contractor
• Common in South FL commercial applications
• Must allow 72-hr cure before tile
• Cost: $150–300 installed (pro only)
• Life: 15–25 years
Bonded Membrane (Schluter KERDI) BEST
• Direct-bond to substrate — no mortar bed required
• ANSI 118.10 compliant (highest standard)
• Compatible with large-format tile directly
• Requires Schluter-branded drain
• Cost: $2.50–5.00/sq ft material
• Life: 30–50 years
Tile-Ready Foam (WEDI / Kerdi-Board) PREMIUM
• Pre-sloped foam panels — no mortar bed pour
• Built-in waterproofing — tile directly on panel
• Fast installation vs. mud bed (1 day vs. 3+ days)
• ANSI 118.10, best for FL concrete slabs
• Cost: $5.00–12.00/sq ft system
• Life: Lifetime of tile installation
Liquid-Applied Membrane (Mapei AquaDefense / RedGard) HVHZ
• Brushed or rolled — 2–3 coats required
• Used over cement board for bonded tile
• NOT for mortar bed applications
• FL HVHZ: R7017 requires breathable WRB on exterior walls — check compatibility
• Cost: $1.00–3.00/sq ft material
• Life: 20–30 years

🪣 Mortar Bed Mixing Guide

Dry-Pack Mix Ratio (FL Standard):
5 parts clean sand : 1 part Portland cement (Type I or II)
Water: just enough to hold shape when squeezed — "sandcastle consistency"
Do NOT use pre-mixed mortar or thin-set for mortar bed

Min Mortar Bed Thickness (ANSI A108.01):
• At drain: 3/4" minimum over liner
• At curb / walls: 1-1/4" minimum
• Reinforcement: 15 lb. felt or metal lath when bed exceeds 1-1/4" in residential FL

Cure Time Before Tile (FL Heat & Humidity):
• Dry-pack bed: 24–72 hours (minimum 24 hrs before walking on)
• Tile installation: minimum 24 hrs after bed achieves 75% strength
• Do NOT let mortar bed dry too fast — cover with plastic in direct FL sun

Curb Height Requirements (FL):

• Standard shower curb: 2" minimum above finished shower floor (FBC R307.2)

• ADA / barrier-free: max 1/2" curb height for roll-in compliance (ADA 2010 §608.7)

• Steam shower curb: 9" minimum above shower floor (to retain steam)

• HVHZ (Miami-Dade / Broward): verify curb height with local AHJ — some coastal areas require 3" min for water intrusion protection

📋 FL Waterproofing Code Requirements
ANSI 118.10 — Bonded Waterproof Membranes

ANSI A118.10 is the gold standard for bonded waterproofing membranes in FL.

• Membrane must achieve 7-day water absorption ≤ 0.2%

• Elongation ≥ 50% at break (critical for FL thermal expansion cycles)

• Adhesive shear strength ≥ 50 psi after water immersion

• Products meeting ANSI A118.10: Schluter KERDI, Mapei AquaDefense, Laticrete Hydro Ban, RedGard, USG Durock Tile Membrane

ANSI A118.12 — Electrically Conductive Mortars (required for heated shower floors)

TCNA Handbook Method B-422 — Reference for bonded membrane over cement board with tile

Florida Building Code — Shower Waterproofing

FBC R307.2 / IPC 417.5:

• All shower receptors and compartments shall be waterproof

• Shower liner (PVC/CPE) shall extend at least 3 inches above the top of the rough curb

• Liner shall be turned up and secured at studs before cement board / backer installation

• Liner test: fill with water to 2" below top of rough curb for 24 hours — zero leakage (FBC R307.2.1)

Inspection Requirements (FBC):

• Pre-pour inspection required before mortar bed pour (liner, drain, liner dam)

• Rough plumbing inspection for drain rough-in

• Some FL counties require flood test inspection by inspector — not just contractor self-certification

HVHZ (High Velocity Hurricane Zone) Requirements

Applies to: Miami-Dade and Broward Counties (and portions of Palm Beach)

• FL Product Approval required for all waterproofing membranes in HVHZ exterior applications

• Interior shower waterproofing: standard ANSI 118.10 products acceptable in HVHZ

• Exterior shower / pool bath walls: must comply with FBC R7017 — weather-resistant barrier (WRB) required behind tile on exterior walls

• HVHZ: all cement board fasteners must be corrosion-resistant (304 SS minimum for shower interiors)

• Miami-Dade Product Control: search productcontrol.miamidade.gov for approved membranes

Steam Shower (HVHZ):

• HVHZ steam showers require vapor barrier on all walls AND ceiling (not just floor)

• Ceiling slope ≥ 1/2" per foot toward wall (prevent steam condensate drip)

• Generator must be FL Product-Approved; outdoor units need 140 mph wind rating

Liner laid flat — no folds or bunching at corners Corner fold must be in one direction; never fold liner on itself — creates leak point

Liner secured to studs at correct height (no staples below waterline) Liner dam — staples or fasteners only above the waterline level
Drain clamping ring bolts finger-tight — liner seated in weep holes Weep holes must be clear and unobstructed before mortar pour
Flood test complete — 2" water held for 24 hours, zero drop FBC R307.2.1 — plug drain, fill to 2" below curb top, mark water level, check after 24 hrs
Drain body level and at correct rough-in height for finished floor Account for mortar bed depth + tile + setting bed; drain collar should finish flush with tile surface
Cement board installed over liner (not under) on walls TCNA — cement board bottom edge holds liner against stud; board bottom should NOT sit in mortar bed

What affects the price?

Pricing depends on the size and layout of your home, the materials and fixtures you choose, your Florida region and local labor rates, and permit fees. Work that is more complex or harder to access generally costs more.

Can I DIY this, or should I hire a licensed plumber?

In Florida, minor maintenance may be DIY, but anything beyond that generally calls for a licensed plumber, and many jobs require a permit and inspection. When a permit, or your main water or drain lines are involved, hire a Florida-licensed plumber.

Does homeowners insurance cover it?

It depends on the cause and your specific policy. Sudden, accidental damage is more often covered than gradual wear-and-tear or maintenance - confirm the details with your insurer.

How long does it take?

Timelines depend on scope - many routine jobs take a few hours to a day, while larger projects run longer. Your licensed plumber can confirm after assessing your home.

Preparation checklist

  • Clear access to the work area and locate your main and fixture shut-off valves.
  • Check with your county or city building department (AHJ) on whether a permit and inspection are required.
  • Note the make, model, or measurements of existing fixtures and pipe materials.
  • Get the scope, total price, warranty, and cleanup terms in writing before work starts.
  • Verify the plumber holds an active Florida license and carries insurance.

Questions to ask your plumber

  • Are you licensed and insured in Florida, and who pulls the permit?
  • Is the quote itemized for parts, labor, permit fees, and disposal?
  • What could change the final price once the work begins?
  • What warranty covers the parts and the labor?
  • How long will the job take, and will my water be shut off?
