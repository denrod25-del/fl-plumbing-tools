🚨 FL Plumbing Emergency Checklist

If you experience a plumbing emergency, follow these steps before calling a plumber:

1️⃣
Locate and close the main shutoff valve — near the water meter at the street or in a utility closet. Closing this stops all water flow into the home immediately.
2️⃣
Turn off your water heater — switch to “pilot” or shut off gas/electricity. Hot water in a burst line can cause scalding and additional structural damage.
3️⃣
Open a faucet to release pressure — this relieves remaining pressure in the lines and reduces further leakage while you assess the situation.
4️⃣
Document the damage thoroughly — photograph and video everything before any cleanup for insurance purposes. Wide shots and close-ups of damage origin are most useful.
5️⃣
Call your CFC licensed plumber — not a handyman. Florida requires CFC or CFC1 licensing for structural plumbing repairs. Call (561) 316-7450 for 24/7 emergency response.
6️⃣
Call your insurance company — sudden and accidental water damage is typically covered. Gradual leaks from lack of maintenance may not be. Report within 24–48 hours.

❓ Florida Plumbing FAQ
How do I know if I have hard water? ▼
Common signs of hard water include white or gray scale deposits around faucets and showerheads, soap that doesn’t lather well, spots on dishes and glassware after washing, and a metallic or chalky taste in tap water. You can confirm with a $10 water hardness test kit from any hardware store, or ask us — we test for free during any inspection visit in the greater South Florida area. Water hardness above 150 mg/L as CaCO₃ significantly shortens pipe life in FL.
Is polybutylene pipe really that dangerous? ▼
Yes — seriously. Polybutylene (PB, gray plastic pipe used 1978–1995) was the subject of a $1 billion class-action settlement (Cox v. Shell Oil, 1995) because chlorine in municipal water causes micro-cracks that accumulate invisibly until the pipe fails — suddenly and completely. Some Florida insurance companies now deny claims on homes with PB pipe or charge significantly higher premiums. We strongly recommend immediate replacement regardless of visible condition or age.
What does a CFC license mean in Florida? ▼
CFC stands for Certified Plumbing Contractor in Florida. Florida Statute 489 requires a CFC or CFC1 license for all structural plumbing work including pipe replacement, water heater installation, and any work that opens walls or floors. Unlicensed plumbers cannot legally pull permits, and unpermitted work can cause insurance claim denials, difficulty selling your home, and code violations. Always verify your plumber’s CFC license at myfloridalicense.com before hiring.
Does homeowner’s insurance cover pipe replacement? ▼
Standard homeowner’s policies cover sudden and accidental water damage — a pipe that bursts unexpectedly. They generally do NOT cover gradual leaks, corrosion, or wear-and-tear pipe replacement. However, the water damage caused by a burst pipe (drywall, flooring, contents) is typically covered after your deductible. The pipe replacement itself is usually your responsibility. Florida homeowners with older pipes should discuss a scheduled maintenance endorsement with their insurance agent.
How long does a whole-house repipe take? ▼
A typical Florida slab home repipe with PEX tubing takes 2–4 days from start to final inspection, including: Day 1 — pipe installation and rough-in; Day 2 — drywall patching and connection; Day 3 — city/county inspection and pressure test; Day 4 — texture, paint touch-up, fixture reconnection. Water is restored within 24 hours in most cases. We work around your household schedule and offer weekend scheduling for working families at no extra charge.
PEX vs. copper — which is better for Florida? ▼
For Florida specifically, PEX has significant advantages: it’s completely immune to the pinhole corrosion that affects copper in hard water areas, it’s significantly less expensive to install (fewer fittings, faster labor), and it’s inherently flexible which matters in slab homes where ground movement is common. Copper remains excellent where water quality is good and is preferred by some buyers for its longevity track record. We recommend PEX-A (expansion type) for most Florida repipes due to superior connection reliability vs. PEX-B or CPVC.

Florida Quick Answers

How much does pipe life expectancy cost in Florida?

On this page, Florida pipe life expectancy estimates run about $200-$350, depending on home size, materials, and project scope. Use the calculator above for a Florida-specific estimate.

What affects the price?

Pricing depends on the size and layout of your home, the pipe materials and fixtures you choose, your Florida region and local labor rates, and permit fees. Work that is more complex or harder to access generally costs more.

Can I DIY this, or should I hire a licensed plumber?

In Florida, minor maintenance may be DIY, but anything beyond that generally calls for a licensed plumber, and many jobs require a permit and inspection. When a permit, gas work, or your main water or drain lines are involved, hire a Florida-licensed plumber.

Does homeowners insurance cover it?

It depends on the cause and your specific policy. Sudden, accidental damage is more often covered than gradual wear-and-tear or maintenance - confirm the details with your insurer.

How long does it take?

Many common jobs are completed the same day, while larger projects can take longer. Your licensed plumber can confirm a timeline after assessing your home.
