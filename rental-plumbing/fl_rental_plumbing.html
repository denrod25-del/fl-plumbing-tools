Key FL Statutes — Plumbing

📋 FS 83.51 — Landlord's Maintenance Obligation ▼
What it says: Landlords must maintain the premises in a habitable condition including: (1) plumbing facilities in working condition; (2) functioning heating facilities; (3) running water; (4) hot water; (5) all delivered utilities. Florida law does not allow "as-is" rental of residential property that violates housing codes.

Plain English: Your landlord is legally required to ensure all plumbing works. This includes toilets, sinks, pipes, water heater, and main water supply. If any plumbing that was present at move-in fails due to normal wear, the landlord must fix it.

How to use it: In your written notice to landlord, cite "Florida Statute 83.51(1)(b)" when referencing plumbing maintenance obligations.
⚖️ FS 83.56 — Landlord Noncompliance; Tenant Remedy ▼
What it says: If the landlord fails to comply with FS 83.51, the tenant must first give 7 days written notice. After that notice period, tenant may: (a) terminate the lease; (b) repair the defect and deduct the cost from rent (up to one month's rent); or (c) withhold rent until repaired.

7-Day Notice requirement: Must be written, delivered to landlord (or their agent), and specifically describe the plumbing issue and the law being violated. Verbal notice is NOT sufficient.

Repair-and-deduct limit: Florida caps repair-and-deduct at ONE MONTH'S RENT per repair event. Cannot be combined across multiple issues to exceed this cap in a single month.
🏠 FS 83.52 — Tenant's Maintenance Obligations ▼
What tenants must do: (1) Comply with sanitation, safety, and housing codes; (2) Dispose of garbage properly; (3) Keep plumbing fixtures clean; (4) Not willfully or negligently damage plumbing; (5) NOT pour grease, foreign objects, or improper materials down drains; (6) Report plumbing issues promptly in writing.

Key point: If a clog is caused by tenant's hair, grease, wipes, or foreign objects, the cost falls on the tenant. Document by reporting issues as soon as you notice them.
🛡️ FS 83.67 — Landlord Retaliation Prohibited ▼
What it says: A landlord may NOT evict, raise rent, reduce services, or threaten a tenant in retaliation for: (1) reporting code violations to a government agency; (2) complaining to the landlord about habitability conditions; (3) exercising any right under FS Ch. 83.

Presumption of retaliation: FL law presumes retaliation if landlord takes adverse action within 60 days of a protected complaint. Burden then shifts to landlord to prove otherwise.

Penalty: Retaliatory action entitles tenant to 3 months' rent or actual damages (whichever is greater), plus attorney's fees.
💰 FS 83.53 — Tenant Right to Withhold Rent ▼
When allowed: After proper 7-day written notice (for non-emergency) or immediate notice (for emergency), if landlord fails to correct a condition that materially affects tenant's health or safety, tenant may withhold rent until condition is remedied.

WARNING: Withholding rent incorrectly can result in eviction. Always consult a FL attorney or legal aid before withholding rent. The notice must be perfect — a defective notice voids your right to withhold.

Emergency shortcut: For true emergencies (no water, sewage backup, flooding), document immediately, notify landlord in writing (text counts if you have record), and call (561) 555-PIPE for emergency service.
📄 Security Deposit & Plumbing Damage ▼
What can landlord deduct? Only TENANT-CAUSED damage beyond normal wear and tear. A landlord CANNOT deduct for: aging pipe failures; worn-out faucets; normal toilet wear; water heater failure due to age.

What they CAN deduct: Broken fixtures due to misuse; intentional damage; clogs from improper disposal; damage caused by not reporting a known leak.

FL deadline: Landlord must return deposit within 15 days (or 30 days with written itemized claim) after tenancy ends. Failure forfeits their right to deduct.
🏢 City/County Codes That Exceed State Minimums ▼
Miami-Dade County: Chapter 17 Housing Code requires annual inspections for multi-family properties. Water pressure must be 40–80 PSI. Landlords must fix plumbing within 24 hours when habitation is threatened.

Broward County: Minimum Housing Standards Code (Chapter 29) adds requirements for pressure-balancing valves in showers, expansion tanks, and anti-siphon devices.

Palm Beach County: Property Maintenance Code requires landlords to maintain all plumbing to IPC standards and provide documentation of annual water heater inspections for units over 5 years old.

Hillsborough County: Chapter 26 adds rental registration requirements. Landlords of 5+ unit buildings must pass annual plumbing inspections or face code enforcement action.
📱 FL Small Claims Court Guide (Plumbing) ▼
Limit: $8,000 per claim (increased from $5,000 in 2024)
Filing fee: $100–$200 depending on claim amount
Where: County court in the county where the property is located

Evidence to bring: Written notices (with proof of delivery), photos/videos, repair receipts, water bills showing spike, text messages, contractor estimates, move-in/out inspection reports

What you can recover: Cost of repairs, water bill increases from landlord's leak, temporary housing costs, property damage from flooding, attorney's fees if landlord acted in bad faith

FL DBPR complaint: For landlords of more than 3 units, you can also file a complaint with FL DBPR (Division of Hotels and Restaurants) for hotels/short-term rentals or with the local housing authority.
🏘️ Condo, Mobile Home & Vacation Rental Rules ▼
Condominiums (FS 718): Generally the condo ASSOCIATION is responsible for plumbing within common walls and serving multiple units. Individual owners responsible for plumbing within their unit. If a pipe in a common wall leaks into your unit, the association pays — but you must document and report immediately.

Mobile Homes (FS 723): Park owner responsible for water and sewer infrastructure. Home owner (or renter) responsible for plumbing within the home itself. Disconnection of water/sewer by park owner without court order is illegal.

Vacation Rentals / Airbnb (DBPR-licensed): Must meet ALL standard residential plumbing codes PLUS Florida Department of Business & Professional Regulation standards. Hot water must be available at all times; bathrooms must have functioning toilets at all times during booking.

How to Write a Proper 7-Day Notice

Use this template as a starting point. Send via certified mail or hand-deliver with a witness.

Dear [Landlord Name], I am writing pursuant to Florida Statute 83.56 to formally notify you of the following plumbing condition at [Property Address]: Issue: [Describe specific plumbing problem] This condition constitutes noncompliance with Florida Statute 83.51(1)(b), which requires you to maintain plumbing facilities in good working condition. Please arrange for repair within seven (7) days of receipt of this notice. Failure to remedy this condition within the required time period may result in my exercising rights under FS 83.56, including terminating this lease, withholding rent, or hiring a contractor to make the repair and deducting the cost from rent (not to exceed one month's rent). Sincerely, [Your Name] Date: [Date] Unit: [Unit Number]

More FAQs

Can my landlord raise my rent because I complained about plumbing? +
No. FL Statute 83.67 prohibits landlord retaliation. If your rent is raised or you receive an eviction notice within 60 days of a plumbing complaint, FL law presumes it is retaliation. You may be entitled to 3 months' rent in damages plus attorney's fees.
Can I just stop paying rent if my plumbing isn't fixed? +
Not without proper notice first. Under FS 83.56, you must send a 7-day written notice to your landlord before withholding rent. Withholding rent without proper notice can result in eviction, even if the landlord is in the wrong. Always consult a FL attorney or legal aid first.
My landlord says the clog is my fault — what do I do? +
Document everything. If the clog is in the main sewer line, it is almost certainly the landlord's responsibility regardless of what went down the drain. Request a camera inspection report — this shows where the blockage is and whether roots, pipe collapse, or debris is the cause. We provide written camera inspection reports.
What if the landlord turns off my water? +
This is illegal in Florida. FS 83.67 prohibits landlords from interrupting utilities (water, electricity, gas) as a method of eviction or coercion, even if you owe rent. This is a criminal act under FL law. Call the police and document it — you may be entitled to immediate restoration plus damages.
Can I hire a plumber and deduct from rent? +
Yes, under FS 83.56, after sending a 7-day written notice and the landlord fails to act, you may hire a licensed plumber and deduct the cost from your next rent payment — up to one month's rent maximum. Keep all invoices and notify the landlord in writing that you are exercising this right.
