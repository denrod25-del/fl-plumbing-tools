📜 FS 553.998 — Backflow Prevention ▼
Florida Statute 553.998 (Backflow Prevention) requires all buildings connected to a potable water supply to have approved backflow prevention devices where cross-connection hazards exist. Key provisions:

• Requires DOH or utility cross-connection control program
• Mandates annual testing of all testable assemblies
• Establishes certification requirements for backflow testers
• Grants utilities authority to shut off service for non-compliance
• Requires property owners to maintain devices in working condition
• Applies to residential, commercial, and industrial properties
• All new construction must include backflow prevention per FL Building Code
📜 FAC 62-555.360 — Backflow Testing ▼
Florida Administrative Code Rule 62-555.360 establishes the technical requirements for backflow prevention and cross-connection control:

• Annual testing of RP, DC, PVB, and SVB assemblies required
• Testing must be performed by FL DEP-certified testers (Certification No. required on report)
• Test reports submitted to water utility within 30 days of test
• Repair or replacement required immediately upon failure
• Re-testing required after repair before returning to service
• Water utilities must maintain records of all test reports for 3 years
• High-hazard assemblies (RP) have priority for enforcement
• AVB devices do not require annual testing (non-testable type)
🌊 FS 373.621 — Reclaimed Water Rules ▼
Florida Statute 373.621 governs reclaimed water use in irrigation systems. Critical cross-connection prevention requirements:

• Reclaimed water systems must be physically separated from potable water
• No direct connection between potable and reclaimed water systems ever permitted
• Air gap or RP assembly required on any potable water backup supply to reclaimed system
• Color-coded piping required: Purple/Pantone 522C for reclaimed water distribution
• Warning labels on all reclaimed water outlets ("DO NOT DRINK")
• Permitted uses: irrigation of lawns, golf courses, agricultural crops, toilet flushing
• 10-foot setback from potable water wells for reclaimed water irrigation
• FL DEP oversees reuse permitting and cross-connection inspections
🏗️ FL Building Code — Irrigation Requirements ▼
Florida Building Code (FBC) plumbing provisions for irrigation and backflow:

• Section 608: Cross-connection control requirements for all plumbing systems
• All irrigation systems connected to potable supply require approved backflow assembly
• Permit required for all new irrigation system installations over 5 zones
• Irrigation permit includes backflow prevention device inspection
• FBC Table 608.1 lists approved backflow devices by hazard degree
• Installation must be by licensed contractor (CFC or CPC license)
• Final inspection required before system is put into service
• Low-volume irrigation (drip) may have reduced backflow requirements per local amendment
🔧 FL Irrigation License Requirements ▼
Florida irrigation contractor licensing under FS 489.1052 and DBPR regulations:

• Irrigation Specialty Contractor (License type: SCC or limited energy/roofing/HVAC does not cover irrigation)
• Licensed Plumbing Contractor (CPC) — can perform all irrigation including connections to potable supply
• Licensed Contractor (CFC) — underground connections to potable water
• Irrigation-only contractor: registered or certified through FDACS (Florida Dept. of Agriculture)
• Backflow tester certification: FL DEP Cross-Connection Control Tester Certification (separate from contractor license)
• Unlicensed irrigation work is a second-degree misdemeanor in FL
• HOA and property managers cannot perform irrigation connections without licensed contractor
• License verification: myfloridalicense.com or DBPR online lookup
📋 FL Irrigation Permit Process ▼
Typical FL county permit process for new irrigation system installation:

Step 1: Hire FL-licensed irrigation contractor
Step 2: Contractor files permit application with county building department
Step 3: Plans submitted (zone layout, water meter sizing, backflow device spec)
Step 4: Permit issued (typically 5-10 business days for residential)
Step 5: Installation by licensed contractor
Step 6: Rough inspection (before burying pipes) — required in most counties
Step 7: Final inspection including backflow device and rain sensor verification
Step 8: Certificate of completion issued

Permit fees: Typically $75-$300 residential, $200-$800 commercial.
Rain sensor requirement: FL Law requires rain/weather sensors on all new irrigation systems (FS 373.62).

FL Rain Sensor Law

FS 373.62: All automatic irrigation systems installed or repaired in FL must have a properly functioning rain sensor or other weather-based shutoff device. Fine: Up to $500 for violation.

Florida Quick Answers

How much does backflow prevention cost in Florida?

On this page, Florida backflow prevention estimates run about $1,200-$3,500, depending on home size, materials, and project scope. Use the calculator above for a Florida-specific estimate.

What affects the price?

Pricing depends on the size and layout of your home, the materials and fixtures you choose, your Florida region and local labor rates, and permit fees. Work that is more complex or harder to access generally costs more.

Can I DIY this, or should I hire a licensed plumber?

In Florida, minor maintenance may be DIY, but anything beyond that generally calls for a licensed plumber, and many jobs require a permit and inspection. When a permit, or your main water or drain lines are involved, hire a Florida-licensed plumber.

Does homeowners insurance cover it?

It depends on the cause and your specific policy. Sudden, accidental damage is more often covered than gradual wear-and-tear or maintenance - confirm the details with your insurer.

How long does it take?

Many common jobs are completed the same day, while larger projects can take longer. Your licensed plumber can confirm a timeline after assessing your home.

Preparation checklist

  • Clear access to the work area and locate your main and fixture shut-off valves.
  • Check with your county or city building department (AHJ) on whether a permit and inspection are required.
  • Note the make, model, or measurements of existing fixtures and pipe materials.
  • Get the scope, total price, warranty, and cleanup terms in writing before work starts.
  • Verify the plumber holds an active Florida license and carries insurance.

Questions to ask your plumber

  • Are you licensed and insured in Florida, and who pulls the permit?
  • Is the quote itemized for parts, labor, permit fees, and disposal?
  • What could change the final price once the work begins?
  • What warranty covers the parts and the labor?
  • How long will the job take, and will my water be shut off?
