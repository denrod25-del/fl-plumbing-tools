⚠️ Well Water Special Considerations

FL well water often contains iron, sulfur, tannins, and bacteria in addition to hardness — a combination system is usually required. Never rely on a single softener for well water treatment.

Recommended Well Water Treatment Stack
PRE-FILTER → IRON FILTER → SOFTENER → UV SYSTEM → POINT OF USE

Stack cost estimate: $2,500–$7,500 installed depending on contamination levels

🧂 Salt Delivery Note

Salt-based softeners require 40–80 lb bags of salt monthly for a typical FL household. Budget $15–$30/month for city water softeners and $25–$45/month for dual-tank systems. Many FL homeowners use salt delivery services — ask your installer for referrals.

📋 FL Regulations & Permits
  • Water softeners do NOT require a permit in most FL counties
  • Salt discharge is regulated in some FL counties — verify before install
  • Well water systems may require a licensed well contractor per FL Stat. §373
  • FL Dept of Health recommends annual water testing for all well owners
  • Broward and Miami-Dade: check saltwater intrusion regs before high-output softeners

📅 Maintenance Schedule
Monthly
  • Check salt level in brine tank (keep ½ to ¾ full)
  • Inspect brine tank for salt bridges or salt mushing
Quarterly
  • Clean brine tank interior — remove salt crust buildup
  • Check resin bed performance (run water hardness test)
  • Inspect and clean venturi valve (common salt clog point)
Annual
  • Professional service inspection and control valve check
  • Resin bed assessment (full replacement every 10–15 years)
  • Control valve rebuild or replacement if wear detected
Well Water — Annual Testing Required
  • Bacteria and coliform (E. coli) test
  • Iron and manganese concentration test
  • pH, hardness, and TDS measurement
  • Nitrates (critical near agricultural areas)

🚰 FL Municipal Tap Water Quality Note

Municipal FL water is safe to drink and meets all federal EPA primary drinking water standards. Hard water is not a health risk — it is a mineral buildup and equipment damage issue. FL's naturally high hardness comes from the Floridan Aquifer limestone formations. Cities are only legally required to meet EPA minimums; they have no hardness removal obligation. Hardness treatment remains the homeowner's responsibility.
