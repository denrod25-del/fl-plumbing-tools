1. Bidet Types Available in Florida: A Complete Overview +

Florida homeowners are increasingly adopting bidet technology, driven by three factors: the aging 65+ population (FL is #1 nationally), post-pandemic toilet paper awareness, and FL's luxury renovation market. Understanding the product categories is essential before any installation.

Non-Electric Bidet Seat

Replaces existing toilet seat. Uses cold water only (or warm if connected to hot supply line). No electricity required. Controls: manual dials or levers on the seat. Features: adjustable spray position, pressure control, self-cleaning nozzle. FL benefit: works during FL power outages common during hurricane season. Price range: $30-200. FL plumber install time: 30-60 minutes.

Electric Bidet Seat (Washlet)

Replaces toilet seat, requires 120V GFCI outlet within 6 ft. Features: heated water, heated seat, warm air dryer, deodorizer, remote or panel control, pressure/temperature/position adjustability, oscillating spray, nightlight. Top FL brands: TOTO Washlet (widely available at FL plumbing suppliers), Brondell Swash, Bio Bidet, Kohler. FL consideration: FL humidity can affect electronic components - choose bidet seats rated for humid environments, IP ratings preferred. Price range: $200-800+.

Handheld Bidet Sprayer (Shattaf)

Simple handheld wand, T-adapter connects to toilet supply. No electricity. Manual pressure control (hand squeeze). FL popularity: very high in FL's diverse communities - standard in Middle Eastern, South Asian, and Southeast Asian households. Extremely common in FL's multicultural South FL market. Price: $25-80. FL tip: install pressure reducer - FL municipal water pressure can be 60-80 PSI, which can cause sprayer blowback without a reducer.

Smart Toilet (Integrated)

Toilet and bidet in one unit. Tankless design, heated seat, remote control, auto-open/close lid, auto-flush, air deodorizer, night light. Top FL brands: TOTO Neorest, Kohler Numi, American Standard Advanced Clean. FL consideration: tankless smart toilets require 120V outlet and adequate water pressure (25 PSI minimum). Requires licensed FL plumber to disconnect/reconnect water supply and set toilet flange. Price range: $800-4,000+.

Standalone Bidet Fixture

Separate porcelain fixture next to toilet. Common in European and luxury FL design. Requires separate floor drain, cold/hot water supply lines, and floor space. Full plumbing rough-in required (FL permit needed). Increasingly rare in new FL construction due to bathroom size and cost. Best for: large master baths with space, luxury FL renovation.

2. FL Building Code Requirements for Bidet Installations +

Florida Building Code (FBC) governs bidet installations across all FL counties. Key requirements vary by installation type.

Electrical Requirements

Any 120V outlet within 6 ft of a water source (sink, toilet, tub, shower) must be GFCI protected per FBC Electrical Code (mirrors NEC 210.8). This applies to bidet seat outlets. AFCI protection may also be required depending on FL county amendments. FL inspection note: if you add an outlet for an electric bidet seat, FL electrical inspector will verify GFCI at time of inspection. Electrical permit required for new outlet.

Plumbing (New Rough-In or Standalone Bidet)

Plumbing permit required, licensed FL plumber required for rough-in. Requirements: floor-mounted bidet requires drain (1.5" minimum drain line), hot and cold supply lines (1/2" minimum), floor drain connection to main waste line. Air gap required between bidet drain and waste system if bidet has integral spray (FL Health Code).

No Permit Required

Swapping toilet seat for non-electric bidet seat, installing handheld sprayer using existing shutoff. These are like-for-like fixture changes with no new rough-in or electrical work.

Permit Required

New electrical outlet, standalone bidet fixture installation, smart toilet installation where new supply or drain work is needed.

FL Code References

FBC Plumbing Section 420 (bidet requirements) - NEC 210.8 (GFCI protection) - FBC Residential Section E3802 (GFCI locations in bathrooms) - FL Statute 489.105 (licensed contractor requirement).

3. FL Water Quality Considerations for Bidet Selection +

FL's water quality varies significantly by region and directly impacts bidet performance and longevity. Understanding your local water quality is essential before purchasing.

South FL (Miami-Dade, Broward, Palm Beach)

Treated surface water (Biscayne Aquifer / Everglades). Chloramine disinfection. High dissolved solids (hardness 150-350 mg/L depending on county). Water hardness causes scale buildup in bidet nozzles and internal valves. Recommended: choose bidet seats with self-cleaning nozzles and scale-resistant internals. Consider whole-home softener or bidet-specific inline filter.

Central FL (Orlando, Tampa Area)

Floridan Aquifer groundwater. Very high hardness (200-450 mg/L) and iron content in some areas. High hardness is the primary enemy of bidet nozzles in Central FL. TOTO and Brondell offer scale-resistant ceramic nozzle options. Inline water filter strongly recommended.

North FL (Jacksonville, Tallahassee)

Varied - some surface water, some groundwater. Generally lower mineral content than S FL but variable. Check with local utility for hardness levels before bidet purchase.

FL Keys (Monroe County)

Desalinated water with low hardness but high sodium content from reverse osmosis process. Less scale buildup but corrosive to certain metals. Use stainless or plastic bidet components in Keys installations.

FL Inline Filter Recommendation

Activated carbon + scale inhibitor filter on cold water supply line before T-adapter. Cost: $20-50 filter + $30-50 plumber install. Replace every 6-12 months. Significantly extends bidet life in FL hard water areas.

4. Installation Process: Step-by-Step for FL Homeowners +

Pre-Installation Check

Plumber verifies existing shutoff valve operates (older FL homes may have stuck shutoffs from mineral buildup - replacement needed before bidet install). Verifies toilet supply line is braided stainless (not plastic, which can crack at T-adapter connection). Verifies toilet flange and wax ring condition (if replacing with smart toilet).

Simple Bidet Seat - 10-Step Process

1. Turn off water at toilet shutoff valve. 2. Flush toilet to empty tank. 3. Remove existing toilet seat (two bolts at rear). 4. Install bidet seat mounting plate (adjusts to fit most FL elongated or round toilets). 5. Slide bidet seat onto mounting plate. 6. Disconnect supply line from fill valve. 7. Install T-adapter (Y-fitting) between shutoff and fill valve. 8. Connect bidet seat supply hose to T-adapter. 9. Reconnect main supply to T-adapter. 10. Turn on water, check for leaks, test bidet functions.

Smart Toilet (Additional Steps)

Old toilet removal, wax ring replacement, toilet flange inspection, new toilet rough-in and bolt installation, tank-to-bowl assembly (or tankless connection), caulk around base, electrical connection to GFCI outlet.

Common FL Installation Issues

Corroded shutoff valve (common in FL homes 20+ years old) - Brittle plastic supply line (replace with braided stainless) - Insufficient water pressure for heated feature activation (check PSI before purchase) - Outlet not GFCI (requires electrician before electric seat can be used).

5. Bidet Seat Sizing for FL Toilets +

Florida's toilet stock is overwhelmingly elongated bowl (standard in FL residential construction since the 1990s), but round bowls exist in older FL homes and some half baths.

Elongated Toilet (Most FL Homes)

Bowl measures approximately 18.5" from seat bolt holes to front of bowl. Elongated bidet seats are designed for this shape - the standard FL purchase. Most bidet brands default to elongated sizing.

Round Toilet

Bowl measures approximately 16.5". Less common in FL but exists in 1960s-1980s FL construction. Must purchase round-specific bidet seat or elongated seat labeled "fits round" (some brands accommodate both shapes).

How to Measure Your FL Toilet for a Bidet

Measure center-to-center distance between seat mounting holes (should be 5.5" for standard - almost all FL toilets). Measure bowl length from mounting holes to front rim. Verify seat overlap does not conflict with tank or lid.

FL Condo Rough-In Note

Many FL condos and older units have American Standard Cadet or Kohler Wellworth toilets with standard 12" rough-in. Smart toilets require verification of rough-in distance before purchase - 10" rough-in toilets (found in some FL condo baths) need specific smart toilet models designed for short rough-in.

6. Water Conservation: FL Bidet Benefits +

FL is a water-constrained state. SFWMD (South Florida Water Management District) and SWFWMD (Southwest Florida Water Management District) actively promote water conservation due to aquifer stress, particularly in S and C FL.

Toilet Paper vs. Bidet Water Usage

Manufacturing one roll of toilet paper requires approximately 37 gallons of water. Average FL household uses 100 rolls/year = 3,700 gallons/year in toilet paper production water. Bidet water use per use: 0.1-0.5 gallons - far less than the manufacturing equivalent.

FL Utility Rebate Potential

Some FL water utilities (Hillsborough County, JEA, OUC) have experimented with water conservation rebate programs. Check your FL utility's current conservation incentives - smart toilets and dual-flush systems sometimes qualify. Contact your local FL utility's conservation department directly.

FL Water Restriction Context

SFWMD and SWFWMD regularly issue lawn watering restrictions during dry season (Nov-May). Bidet water use is indoor use (not subject to outdoor restrictions) and is minimal relative to the conservation value delivered.

8. Troubleshooting Bidet Issues in FL +

Weak Spray

(1) Water pressure too low - check home pressure (should be 40-80 PSI for bidet); (2) clogged nozzle from FL hard water scale - run self-clean cycle, soak nozzle in vinegar; (3) kinked supply hose behind toilet; (4) partially closed T-adapter.

No Warm Water

(1) Electric seat - no power to outlet (check GFCI reset); (2) warm water tank empty (small tank on electric seats - wait 30 seconds between uses); (3) water too cold entering home (rare in FL but possible in N FL winters).

Bidet Won't Turn On

(1) GFCI outlet tripped - press reset button on outlet or check bathroom GFCI outlet which may control multiple outlets; (2) power cord loose; (3) seat sensor not detecting occupant (some seats require body weight to activate - child may not trigger sensor).

Leaking at T-Adapter

(1) Overtightened plastic fittings (crack from excess torque); (2) under-tightened (insufficient thread engagement); (3) supply line damaged during install. Fix: turn off shutoff valve, inspect, replace T-adapter or supply line as needed.

Scale Buildup on Nozzle (FL Hard Water)

Monthly citric acid or white vinegar soak. Some FL bidet users add inline softening cartridge. Do NOT use bleach or harsh cleaners on bidet nozzles - degrades plastic and contaminates the spray water.

Seat Rocking or Unstable

Mounting plate bolts loosened. Tighten hand-tight only - over-torquing cracks porcelain toilet seat mounting holes. This is a common FL repair mistake in older porcelain. If porcelain is cracked, toilet replacement may be needed.

    Who Can Pull a Permit in FL?

    Per FL Statute 489.105, only a licensed electrical contractor (EC) or licensed plumbing contractor (CFC) may pull permits for electrical and plumbing work respectively in FL. Homeowner exception applies only to owner-occupied single-family dwellings, and homeowner must personally perform the work.

    What affects the price?

    Can I DIY this, or should I hire a licensed plumber?

    Does homeowners insurance cover it?
