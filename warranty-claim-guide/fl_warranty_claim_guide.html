FL-Specific Warranty Denial Reasons

Florida's hard water, chloramine treatment, and high humidity create unique denial vulnerabilities not common in other states. Know these before you file.

🔴 Scale Damage — No Maintenance Records
South FL's hard water (10–18 GPG in Collier, Lee, Miami-Dade) deposits calcium scale inside tankless heat exchangers at an accelerated rate. Manufacturers require annual or biannual descaling. Without service records — receipts, photos, or technician invoices — the brand will attribute failure to "owner neglect" and deny the claim.
Fix: Keep every service receipt. Take dated photos of your flush kit connection annually. If you DIY flush, log dates in a notes app with a photo of the descaling solution bottle and flush setup.
🔴 Improper Installation — Missing FL Permit
Florida Statutes require a permit for water heater replacement in most counties. Many manufacturers explicitly state in warranty documents that installation without required permits voids the warranty. If a plumber did an "unpermitted swap" to save money, your warranty may already be void from day one.
Fix: Pull the permit history for your property at your county's building department website. If installation was unpermitted, some counties allow after-the-fact permits — contact a licensed plumber immediately.
🔴 Unlicensed Installation
Most warranty documents require "installation by a licensed plumber." Florida requires a CFC (Certified Plumbing Contractor) for water heater installation. If installed by a handyman, general contractor, or unlicensed person, the manufacturer can deny all claims regardless of failure mode. This is the most common reason FL claims are denied outright.
Fix: Verify your installer's license at myfloridalicense.com before filing. If unlicensed, contact the manufacturer's escalation line — some will honor the claim if failure is clearly a manufacturing defect (e.g., first-year failure, known defect series).
🟡 High Water Pressure — No PRV / Expansion Tank
FL municipal systems often deliver pressure above 80 PSI, especially in newer subdivisions or during morning off-peak. Manufacturers require water pressure to be regulated to 80 PSI or less. A pressure-reducing valve (PRV) and expansion tank are required by Florida Plumbing Code on closed systems. High-pressure tank failure is denied as "improper operating conditions."
Fix: Check pressure at hose bib with a $15 gauge before filing. If above 80 PSI, have a plumber install a PRV + expansion tank ($350–$600) before filing the claim — many adjusters check for this first.
🟡 Wrong Temperature Setting — Legionella Override
Some FL homeowners reduce water heater temperature below 120°F to slow chloramine-driven corrosion or to comply with scalding prevention for elderly residents. However, tankless manufacturers may use low-temperature operation as a basis to deny claims related to sediment or bacterial fouling, arguing "non-standard operation."
Fix: Maintain minimum 120°F setting (DOE recommended). Use a tempering/mixing valve at fixtures to prevent scalding if children or elderly are present rather than lowering the heater temp.
🟡 Water Quality Out of Spec — FL Well Water
FL well water with H₂S (sulfur), very high iron (>3 ppm), extreme hardness (>25 GPG), or low pH (<6.5) is outside the rated operating parameters for most water heaters. Manufacturers can deny warranty claims citing water quality. This is common for rural FL homes on untreated well water.
Fix: Get a water quality test before installing. If water quality is out of spec, install appropriate pre-treatment (softener, iron filter, neutralizer). Keep the test results to prove your water was treated to manufacturer specifications.
🟢 Manufacturing Defect — Strong Claim
Tank liner failure, thermocouple failure in the first 2 years, control board failure, heat exchanger pinhole within 3 years of installation, and anode rod failure within 12 months are typically covered without dispute. These failure modes are clearly manufacturing-related and manufacturers handle them efficiently.
Document everything: photos, failure date, technician diagnosis report, and keep all original purchase and installation paperwork. These claims are usually approved within 5–10 business days with proper documentation.

Step-by-Step Warranty Claim Process
1
Decode the serial number to confirm manufacture date (use the Warranty Lookup tab). Verify you're still within the warranty period — warranty runs from manufacture date OR installation date, whichever is later (keep installation receipt).
2
Get a licensed plumber's written diagnosis. Most manufacturers require a licensed technician's failure diagnosis report before processing a claim. The report must state the specific failure mode (e.g., "tank liner perforation," "heat exchanger scale blockage," "faulty gas valve").
3
Gather your documentation package: serial number, model number, purchase receipt, installation invoice (with license number), permit number, maintenance service records, and technician diagnosis report. Missing any of these = claim delay or denial.
4
Call the manufacturer's warranty line (not the retailer). Request a warranty claim number and file within 30 days of failure. Most brands: Rheem 1-800-432-8373 · AO Smith 1-800-527-1953 · Bradford White 1-800-523-2931 · Navien 1-800-519-8794 · Rinnai 1-800-621-9419.
5
Request a factory-authorized service technician if the manufacturer disputes the diagnosis. Brands are required to send a factory-authorized tech within a reasonable time. Do not accept "send it in for evaluation" for an installed water heater — that applies to portable products only.
6
If denied, escalate to the Florida Attorney General. FL Statute 672.313 (warranty of merchantability) and the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act provide additional consumer protections. File a complaint at myfloridalegal.com. Manufacturers frequently reverse denials when an AG complaint is filed.
7
When to stop fighting and replace: If the unit is >75% through its warranty period, fighting a denial may cost more in time than the unit is worth. Get a licensed replacement quote and compare to your estimated claim recovery timeline.

Replace vs. Fight — FL Decision Framework
When to just replace: If the unit is 80%+ through its warranty period, is a tank-style heater over 8 years old in South FL's chloramine water, has already had one repair, or the denial reason is legitimate (scale, no permit, unlicensed install) — replacement is usually the better financial decision. A new PEX-piped system with a new warranty beats a contested repair on an aging unit.
